If At First You Don't Succeed...

by "Rand Brookings" ( Sunny Nelson and John Sabotta )

 

 

“What?”

Glen MacDonald couldn't believe what he had just heard from his supervisor, Davis Fenster.

“Yes” grated Fenster “you heard me, MacDonald! The Subatomic Energy Division is being shut down, effective as of tomorrow!”

MacDonald stepped forward, looming over the shorter Fenster and his desk. MacDonald was six-foot-four, three hundred pounds of muscle that his fighting Scots ancestors would have admired. His kilt bore the ancestral MacDonald tartan. His ham-like hands gripped the edge of the desk as he glared at Fenster. The autovidphone buzzed, but neither man moved.

“Listen” said Glen evenly, through his teeth, “The Subatomic Energy Division was the best thing this company had going for it! We were on the verge of a breakthrough that will solve the problem of free energy for the next thousand years! And now you throw all that away! All I want to know is why!”

Fenster seemed to shrink. “Policy from upstairs. ” he muttered.

Upstairs! MacDonald knew what that meant. He had run-ins with the double-domes before. “The physicists” he snarled.

“They say the figures don't add up, Glen!” Fenster pleaded. “They say you're building a perpetual motion machine!”

“Have they ever turned a valve or milled a hollowground Jorgenson cube?” demanded MacDonald. “Those scientists tear down the work of men like me because they look down their noses at us engineers! We deal with physical reality every day! Toe to toe with Mother Nature! They think everything is an equation!”

The autovidphone buzzed again. “Call for you from Science Central” said Patrice, Fenster's lovely, but intelligent secretary. She saw Glen in the office, and for a microsecond, their glances locked.

Glen had had enough. He turned on his heel and stalked out. As he passed through the outer office, he saw Patrice again. “Giving you a hard ride, Glen?” she asked. Glen approved of the way her syntho-Angora sweater rode out her own curves.

“Yeah” he replied. His steely grey eyes softened for a moment. “Don't worry about me, kid.”

“Keep slugging, big boy.” she advised. “Silver lining in every cloud, you know.”

Glen smiled for a moment. He would have liked to stay, but he knew there was work to do now.

Glen MacDonald walked through the ultramodern glassite and steelio corridors of the Technicorp Administration Hexacomplex Five. His big powerful strides carried him along at twice the speed of the scientists and bureaucrats crowding past him, his square-jawed, red- haired head visible above the sea of bald pates and sunken chins.

Jim Harcourt approached him, visibly agitated. “Is it true, Glen? They're shutting us down?”

Glen studied Jim for a split second. Harcourt was one of his best assistants - a topflight mechanical engineer. Even though Jim was black, Glen reflected, that didn't stop him from being treated exactly like every other member of the team. No irrational racial prejudice in engineering!

“Yes, they are, Jim. Fenster gave me the bad word just now.” “Fungo!” Jim swore. “Those sheet chewers! Why?” Glen laid a giant hand on Jim's shoulder. “The physicists put the Indian sign on us, Jim. Said the numbers didn't tickle their fancy the right way.”

Jim shook his head. “There's more than just those pen-pushers at work here.”

Glen nodded his great head slowly. Neither of them had to say a word. They both knew that the physicists were just the paid lapdogs of the world-wide managerialist conspiracy. All over the world, accountants and department heads called the shots and sent out the directives that kept their stodgy, unimaginative tyranny in operation. They liked conventional science because they knew it could never challenge their power - or their appropriations. Glen, a supreme individualist and all-around maverick was constantly jousting with their one-track minds and their bean-counting ways. All engineers did, of course, but Glen MacDonald had the wild, fey Highlands blood coursing through his veins, and they were afraid of him - afraid of his ham-like fists as well as his keen, canny mind.

“Come on, Jim” Glen's deep voice rumbled “We haven't played all the cards in our hand yet. Lets get back to Energylab and talk to the rest of the crew.”

Felix Mahlstadt looked out the window at the Technicorp rocket- plane pad, fixated on the flashing green lights that led into the distance like some sacred road. He adjusted one of his vanadium cufflinks and sighed, returning to his desk. The last honey-colored daylight was falling across the floor.

Felix bent to pick up a pen as the autovidphone buzzed. He answered it.

“Felix?” Mike Kopachevsky's thick voice came across the line. “Hello, Mike. What is going on?”

“Well, it's really strange... There has been some kind of security leak or something, because a whole bunch of people have gathered out at the front gate chanting and yelling about the Subatomic Energy Project.”Kopachevsky sounded incredulous.

“Chanting? How many people? Mike, are you pulling my leg?” Felix demanded. “Is this because of that jab about sin x/n = 6 in the newsletter? You know I didn't mean...”

“No. This is real. Security is trying to find out how it slipped. Anyhow, public relations is sending someone to talk to them but it might be good if the head of R&D was there too.”

“You're right Mike. What absurd positions one gets into when promoted! I'll head down as soon as possible.”

“That's a good idea, Felix. It will also keep you from having to explain the situation to that crazy MacDonald,” Mike chuckled. “I just talked to Davis Fenster and he said MacDonald was on his way out. He might be on his way here.”

“Oh!” Felix laughed. “Well it was a matter of time. Those engineers never let go of anything. They are like little garbage magnets! I will talk to you later, Mike.”

“See you Felix,” Mike hung up.

Felix walked over to the mirror, slicked his black hair back with his thin, distinguished hand, and straightened his tie. He relished the opportunity to play at “technocrat” before an audience and even, perhaps, vid cameras. On the other hand, it annoyed him that MacDonald was causing trouble again. Those stupid technicians! What they lacked in brains they made up for in cheap machismo and dog-like persistence. This infinite energy foolishness had always appeared under various guises and would certainly continue to do so, but to be its victim was embarrassing. Of course, it was understandable... Felix wouldn't be an engineer for all the poly-creds in the world, even if he didn't have to drink their cheap, evil smelling beer.

He left the office and strolled over to the secretary's desk, but Mona had gone home. He went to the file cabinet and found the latest bulletin from MacDonald's benighted project, and read it as he rode first down the elevator and then on the sleek, silvery personnel conveyance to the front gate. The bulletin was written mostly by that monomaniac MacDonald himself. It described the design of their latest gadget, the cyclo-gradient dyad separator. He snickered. As he turned the page however, Felix's face darkened. That idiot Calhoun was trying to use Felix's own results in ergodic Venn-lattices to model their “pion resonance sub-factor”. That was the final straw.

As he walked out of Facility 1 toward the gate he was approached by a beautiful but uncompromising girl with long chestnut hair and tinted vis-o-glasses. who extended her hand. “Are you Dr. Mahlstadt? I'm Arlene Cooper, from P.R.” she smiled at him.

He shook her hand and smiled back. “Yes, I'm Mahlstadt. I'm glad you're here. Do you know what these people want exactly?”

“Well, the group seems mostly comprised of little old ladies, and what I suppose are college students and political cranks. They say that subatomic energy is unnatural,” she explained as they walked across the lawn together.

Felix laughed out loud. “Well that's true at least! It's an impossibilty!”

“Where do you suppose the security leak occurred?” she asked as they walked across the Asfalt. “Or is that too hush-hush?”

Felix looked at her. She was dressed in a fashionable brick-red caftan and black leggings. Arlene had the assured self confidence characteristic of all marketing and public relations workers. Perhaps it came from knowing that her neck was never on the line, that she was just there to help other people escape the guillotine.

“I suspect that MacDonald has been flapping his great maw in some local bar,” he replied.

Finally they passed through the gate and saw a crowd of emotional young people and resolute old people, some carrying signs saying “Enough S.E.D.” or “Down with Atomic Playboys!” and even one besmirching Dr. Mahlstadt personally.

Arlene waved the crowd down and said “We are here to make an announcement! We appreciate your concern!” The mob quieted somewhat and she said “Here is Dr. Felix Mahlstadt, head of research and development for Technicorp, to explain the situation.” Amid a few boos and a little more chanting, Felix stepped forward. “Good afternoon! I'd like to thank you all for coming down today; it's concerned citizens like you that make democracy work.”

He was rudely interrupted by several students chanting “Feed the people, not the reactor!” They bore an enormous banner picturing a clumsily scrawled mushroom cloud. A middle aged, professorial-looking man started sputtering “How dare you talk about democracy, when you criminals are holding our country hostage to your...” Felix shrugged and continued. “At any rate, there seems to be some concern about a 'Subatomic Energy Division' I believe it was called, and I'd like to assure you that here at Technicorp no research is being done in that direction. I don't know where this rumor came from but that is all it is, a rumor. To be honest, I don't believe this subatomic energy stuff works anyway.” He smiled his “good-natured” smile. “Some researcher told the newspapers about the idea before he even told the rest of the scientific world. I say its a publicity stunt, and we at Technicorp do not involve ourselves in such things.”

Suddenly a rather unkempt young man lunged at him. Felix jumped back in alarm as two company guards rushed forward and wrestled the man to the ground. Arlene stepped forward quickly. “Dr. Mahlstadt will not be answering any questions at this time!” She and Felix ducked back in the gate.

“Power to the Net!” shouted Arvid Schiller, watching the demonstration on his bootlegged vidreciever. He manipulated the keys on an ancient Commodore 64 computer he wore on a strap around his neck. The picture on the screen was overlaid with the bright day-glo orange logo of the Neo-Anarchoids - a cartoon skull with a computer chip stuck through it.

The dingy, drab little room in the abandoned glassite processing facility was filled with people dressed in black canvas and ragged T- shirts with faded corporate logos. This was “Nagoya”, the quasi- headquarters of the Neo-Anarchoids - outlaw hackers and 8-bit cowboys all. Arvid bounced the logo around the vidscreen as the others cheered and hooted. The Neo-Anarchoids wanted to have the logo overlay all the vidscreens throughout the world, but months of fevered hacking with their souped-up Commodore 64's and TI 99/4A decks had failed to crack the communication companies code.

“Fung on you, Mahlstadt!” screamed “Creamball” Johanson, one of the Neo-Anarchoids' most pertinacious hackers. Creamball was a short boy with crater acne and a tee-shirt bearing a puff-ink image of Mussolini, but using a virt-reality headset he had once hacked his way deep into the virtual world of a “Burger Castle” order-taking computer. He had hit a patch of black ice surrounding the sales-tax database and lain paralyzed with ECG-shock for three days.

A knock sounded at the door. “Password!” demanded Arvid, as the rest tensed. Could it be Technicorp security? “Uh...'Joseph Chamberlain'” came the muffled response from behind the door. “Check him!” snarled Arvid, motioning to a fat, frizzy-haired girl with an Atari 800. She found a tape cassette under a pile of dirty laundry, loaded it into the cassette drive. Minutes passed as the Neo- Anarchoids security program loaded in. She put on the virt-harness and her eyes rolled up into her head. She was, Arvid knew, being plunged into a blue, bitty vert world with big orange letters. Her fingers flew over the keyboard. The person outside coughed and shuffled his feet. “S'all right” she gasped as Johanson tore off the harness. Arvid immediately keyed in the door combination on his deck and the lock opened.

A tall thin young man stumbled in, still dressed in Technicorp lower-management blue serge. “What's going on, Phil?” Arvid demanded, as the rest gathered around the new arrival. “Technicorp's shutting down SAE.” replied Phil “I got a hardcopy of the memo from a wastebasket. But I hear MacDonald's not going to stand for it!”

“They can't shut it down” snarled Arvid again, wiping a lank strand of greasy black hair from his forehead. “We need the Subatomic Project for our master anarchy plan! It's the only way to bring down this oppressive power structure!”

“Everything's being scrapped and carted off tomorrow morning, Arvid. I saw the gluon cutters being brought up to the main gate!”

Arvid paused a moment. He turned on the fat girl. “Denise, have you decoded those punch cards Phil swiped from the SAE computer yet?”

Denise nodded. “I've made up the preliminary access cards already, Arvid. All we need is the final access code that the engineers are using!” Her voice reflected both her longing for Arvid's hot nihilistic love and her existential despair of ever experiencing it. Stories she had heard from a VIC-20 groupie were both terrifying and strangely compelling.

“Right!” Arvid's eyes flashed “Tonight's our only chance! We must get into Technicorp tonight and get into that computer!”

Inside the great central energy facility a dispirited group gathered around the mighty steelio container vessel of the Subatomic Energy Project. They were all project engineers, normally a high-spirited, beer- drinking lot, men who worked hard, sweating the details, and played hard, sweating like pigs. Now they saw the failure of all they had tried to accomplish staring them in the face. Strong men with chiseled features bit back bitter curses as they reached for another can of cheap, evil smelling beer.

“Fung it all!” shouted Rory Calhoun, a big bear of a man. “Let 'em all go to schenectady if they don't want infinite power for nothing!” A deep murmur of agreement greeted these words.

Suddenly, all heads turned, all voices stilled. Glen McDonald and Jim Harcourt had entered the chamber. McDonald strode into their midst with a self-assured power. He clapped a big hand on Calhoun's shoulder. “Mankind needs what we've got, Rory,” McDonald rumbled “Would we be keeping faith with our children and our children's children if we let the small-minded and the envious stop us now?” Rory hung his grizzled head. “You're right, Glen. I don't know why I said that. ”

“Good man” said Glen, and then turned to the rest of the engineers. “Listen, men. G.Harry Stine said decades ago that progress was like a curve, and that someday everybody on earth would be commanding the power of a sun for their personal use! We're not there yet, and why?” He paused. “You know why as well as I do! It's not in the interest of the big boys that men have the freedom of infinite power! And the big-brain scientists stick to their rules and laws they get out of books, while we engineers do the real work! We're on the verge of ultimate energy here! There's only one way to save our work - we have to get the system running tonight!”

“But Glen” Jim said “we'll need months of adjusting power settings to find the right harmonic balance - we haven't got an erg of output from the system yet! I know we're close, but...”

“It's our only chance!” boomed Glen. “Either we make it tonight or we - and mankind - have lost the biggest race of them all!”

“I can't believe it! One of them tried to attack you?” Mike Kopachevsky was sitting on Felix's desk spinning a colorful cardboard dodecahedron between his massive index fingers. “That's just nuts! What a bunch of fobbers...”

Felix waved his hand in annoyance. “All of this trouble for one delusional engineer. I think we should fire him, because I am sure the security leak was his fault.”

“Perhaps firing him would not be the most useful thing to do, Dr. Mahlstadt, if you don't mind my saying so,” Arlene put in.

“What do you mean, Ms. Cooper?” Mike turned to her.

“Well, if this subatomic energy stuff works, then he'll take it somewhere else, maybe to Inter-Atomix. And, conversely, if it doesn't work, then he'll just head right off to secure an interview on the Harry Nickerson show or something and, well, the publicity is hardly something you need now.”

“You are right, of course. We must keep him here where we can keep control of him. However, we should get together Vandenloos and Wang and anyone else we can find to go shut down their project tonight. As department head, I feel that I should have a word with MacDonald personally. But I would feel better with a couple of allies along.” Dr. Mahlstadt laughed and walked over to the bar.“Perhaps I'm just nervous from having been attacked by one lunatic already today.”

The burly Kopachevsky stood up and walked toward the door. “It's appropriate for the physicists and mathematicians to shut the thing down anyway. After all, the engineers create the end product which begins with our work, so I think we should take action now to bring this 'product' of theirs to the only conceivable end it could have - if only in the interest of symmetry. I'll round up whichever Review Committee members are still here.” He walked out.

“Do you wish to accompany us to Energylab?” Felix asked the girl sitting in his chair. “I suspect that you might provide a needed voice of reason.”

“Yes, I'll come. Perhaps this can all go on my resume later on.”

It was dark as the 1981 Pontiac Sunbird pulled up to the Technicorp main gate. Rain was coming down now on the hood of the car, the security booth, and the last of the demonstrators, two bedraggled girls under an opened sleeping bag. They yelled insults at the Sunbird.

Behind the wheel, Phil admonished Arvid and Denise to stay down on the floor and stay quiet while he showed his ID to the guard. “Fungo! What do you take us for, Speak & Spell jockeys or somethin' ?” Arvid sneered, before Phil shushed him and rolled down the window.

Soon they were headed toward Parking Bay 5, and the two hidden Neo-Anarchoids risked sitting up. As they drove past Bay 2, the parking for the R&D department, Phil noted that Dr. Mahlstadt's old Volkswagen Thing and MacDonald's aircar were still there.

“That means we might have a chance at that fobber first hand!” Denise exclaimed. “They won't keep us down any more, huh Arvid? Now it's our turn! Let the Kids run things for a change!”

“Yeah, it's gonna be Mega!” Arvid agreed, scratching inside his T- shirt. “This place is bigger than I thought, Phil.”

“This isn't even half of it...these fungers have their own rocket- plane launch!” Phil told them. “Oh, there's Bay 5. We're there now.”

MacDonald was pacing. “Mass into energy” he muttered “Subatomic mass into subatomic energy through harmonic vibrations in the space-time continuum! That's the final equation! The key! How to unlock it in time?”

The others stood back, not daring to interrupt the giant engineer. Jim and Rory were at the GENIAC terminal, feeding in punch cards, working out thousands of possible vibration-patterns to feed into the great banks of resonating bi-polar attenuators looming above them, glistening black plastika stacks reaching up into the dim heights of the ceiling.

The attenuators worked on the pulsed bi-polar resonation principle, and their “vibration wave-front” was aimed directly into the three-story square steelio subatomic chamber, where the vast cthonic energies of the subatomic “multi-universes” would be liberated to heat syntho-sodium in great pipes, providing unlimited power!

Jim came up to MacDonald, looking worried. Inner doubt seemed to be gnawing at him. “Glen” he said quietly. “I've thought of a moral angle to this whole project.”

Glen MacDonald turned his level gaze on his old pal. “Spit it out, Jim. We're all engineers here.”

“Well, we know that subatomic particles consist of tiny little galaxies, solar systems and planets, all whirling through the subatomic void. Sagan's Law proves that all of these planets must have intelligent life on them, and advanced civilizations with cultures of their own. Our power plan works by disrupting the subatomic suns and smashing the subatomic planets to liberate the potential base energy charge within! Do we really have the right to destroy these other universes? For all we know, a duplicate Glen MacDonald and Jim Harcourt might be on one of them right now.”

Glen, for the first time in many hard, grueling hours, broke into a smile.

“Jim, you've answered your question by asking it!” Glen clapped a friendly hand on Jim's shoulder. “If there were a Glen MacDonald and a Jim Harcourt on one of these planets, or creatures sufficiently like us to worry about, they would have long before now discovered our plans and found a way to make themselves known to us. Well, they haven't and so that's that. No need to worry about it, Jim! I know these moral angles can be real brain-busters, but I find all it takes is hard straight thinking for a few moments to clear them right up!”

Greatly relieved, Jim returned to his work at the GENIAC terminal.

The great machines throbbed and hummed in the huge hall. From a nearby darkened alcove the engineers - even Glen MacDonald - looked like ants crowded around the terminals.

Inside the alcove crouched the contingent of Neo-Anarchoids. Arvid stealthily withdrew the parabolic disk microphone he had been aiming at the group. He stuffed the microphone into his backpack and popped the tape cassette of the recording out of the specially modified Fisher-Price “My First Tape Deck” attached to his belt.

“Got every word!” Arvid sneered, slipping the cassette into one of the pockets of his stained “cyber-vest”. “Sub-atomic universe, huh! Those dopey engineers are all goobered! The future belongs to Cyber Anarchy!”

Denise looked longingly at Arvid's feral little face and stringy black goatee. In the half-light, for a moment, she thought of Arvid as a cross between Robert Anton Wilson and Charles Manson. Her loins ached...

“Shhh!” hissed Phil frantically. “We've got to get out of here!”

“Yeah, yeah, you skanky corpo wanger! Don't tell me what to do! Everybody over into the next exit!”

The little group of terrorists slipped into the darkened side hallway. They crept down the corridor, past the empty offices. Phil stopped at one door. “This is it.” he whispered. “This is where the alternate terminal station is.”

Arvid produced a set of lockpicks and worked at the door lock for twenty minutes, before finally taking out a crowbar from his pack and prying the door open. Inside, the heat and noise were almost unbearable. The office actually faced onto the giant building where the GENIAC operated.

Through the glassite windows of the control chamber, they could see mile upon mile of sliding ribbons of steelio, shifting back and forth, grinding and pounding away at hundreds of calculations every second. The machine was working on harmonic combinations for MacDonald and his group. The terminals were blank metal boxes with input and output slots. Punch-card devices were at each desk, and a punch-card reader stood in the corner.

Arvid popped the tape back into the cassette machine and replayed the engineer's conversation. All listened intently. In the background, as Glen and Jim were talking, they could hear Rory working at one of the machine room terminals. “2234”, they heard him say faintly.

“That's it!” cried Denise “The access code!”

She took a blank card from a dispenser on the wall, shoved it into a card puncher and pushed in the numbers “2”, “2”, “3” and “4”. She took the punched card and fed it into the input slot of the terminal nearest her.

A row of green light bulbs flashed on the wall opposite them, and an ACCESS CONFIRMED panel lit up.

They were in.

Arvid leaned forward. His thin, wasted little body shook with excitement. This was what he lived for, balancing on the edge of the big curve. Beating the dead hand of the oppressive System! The kids knew what the Gutenberg-oriented adults did not - that the cyber-world of the future was theirs to take. The cyber-kids, the skater gangs, the carbon tet trippers and the Neo-Anarchoids were surfing the electronic wave, flowing with the Big Curve! Something Tim Leary had said back in the dinosaur days seemed appropriate right now, if only he could remember it.

“We're ready, Arvid.” Denise said. Without a word, Arvid pulled the virt-harness out of the backpack and plugged it in to the socket on the side of the terminal. He put it on and looked at the others “The funging engineers don't have our outlaw virt-tech.” he said. “They're trying to find the answers the old-style way, through a terminal. We'll get it before they do! Then it's liberation of energy for Neo Anarchy!”

With a savage jab of his thumb, Arvid turned the harness on.

Mike Kopachevsky made his way through Science Central, occasionally looking into the offices of the department's crack science staff. His feet sounded heavily in the quiet of the halls. He whistled an old Russian song as he went and smiled with glee at the thought of the engineers with their infinite water wheel.

Turning a corner, Mike heard voices coming from an open door across the hall, the door to Snack Shop 5.

”...something about harmonic resonance in the subatomic manifold. I'd never heard such nonsense! I thought 'Does he know who he's talking to? Can he really think he can snork me?' but I guess he did, poor guy.” It was Joe Wang, a recent addition to the R&D group and an ace solid- state man. Mike stuck his head in the door and saw that with Wang was Erich Vandenloos, a hotshot young mathematician who'd been working on the Discrete Ring Computability Project.

The snack shop was a gleaming modern affair, with glassite tables and steelio-and-black leatheron chairs. The table at which the scientists sat was piled with ersatz-tofu pocket sandwiches, chips and sugarine snacks from the Dial-a-Snack set-up in the corner.

Vandenloos smoked while Wang recounted his favorite tales of the engineers. Bartok chamber music came across the quadrophonic hi-fi system. “Hello, gentlemen,” Mike walked in. “Hard at work on subatomic energy I see.”

They laughed and greeted him. “Have some Koffee, Mike, and tell us what's new down at the Muon-permittivity lab,” Wang suggested.

“Actually, I'm here on business for a change... uh, Wang, would you hand me one of those Pep-cakes... You see, those bozos down at Energylab aren't giving up. They're at it down there tonight. Furthermore, we think MacDonald told somebody outside about the Division and there was a near riot right outside the gate. Some doped-up beatnik tried to assault Felix!”The other men gasped.

Mike continued “So Felix and I thought we'd head down there tonight and stop them before they hurt themselves, and we thought it would be better to have some other guys from 'Central along.”

“You can count on us, Mike-O,” laughed Wang. “We'll show those cloddys something!”

Vandenloos smiled enigmatically and they all finished their snacks and Koffee.

Arvid was spinning down into a dark well.

It was the familiar but exciting, and somewhat sickening, feeling of leaving the world of the straights and the fobbers for his world. If only everything could be as hype as this! But then it would be, once the master anarchy plan was complete.

His thoughts of victory were cut short by the need to find his bearings. Looking down at his virt-body, Arvid saw his long, thin legs wrapped in shiny charcoal leatheron and boots with chromanium shin- guards. Across and around his torso was a network of straps and wires and cords. Once Cyber-Anarchy was in place, he'd look like this all the time, and trash that 'Mr. Bubble' T-shirt.

Arvid looked around, and was annoyed to discover that he was in an area that looked like a junior high school gym. The floor wasn't wood, however, but shimmering yellow. Like any gym floor, it was covered by circles and lines, with a big 'T' in the center circle. The whole scene was bathed in an eerie green light which came from two signs, one on either side, reading “EXIT.”

Underneath these signs were door icons; there were also two more door icons with no signs. Above, the place just stretched away farther than Arvid could see.

Suddenly it dawned on him that those marks on the floor were like a map to the whole system. That 'T' was the main memory bank! But where was he on this map?

Arvid puzzled over this, and then realized that he was just where he was standing! The map was to show him where to go, so it must start where he started!

He peered around and decided after some thought not to go through the “exit.” Instead he went toward one of the other doors. He passed through the icon and found himself in a blank white world with a black rectangle some distance away. He tried to go to the rectangle, but it was like walking on a slippery surface and he kept losing his balance.

But eventually he made it to the dark spot, which proved to be another door icon. As he passed through, he noted that he was now in a higher traffic area. Red, spherical info-packets slid past. There was a loud clackety sound coming from somewhere. That made sense; it was the card reader, and he would be near the I/O portal.

He decided to follow the red packets, thinking that they led further into the system. The spheres led him on a circuitous journey through the giant cyber-technoverse. This was the best curve he'd ever ridden and at this point, he didn't care whether he lived or died because this was it, the ultimate mega-hack!

Eventually, he came to a large planar area in which the spheres one by one dropped into depressions in the floor. Far away, other spheres were popping back up and going out, presumably to the stupid engineers' terminal. This had to be the central processing unit! All of this information entered through those holes.

Arvid went over to one and jumped in. He found himself surrounded by colorful grids, like plaid, intermingling in the intricate dance of computation. It was beautiful...but it didn't make any sense! Arvid realized that this machine was more advanced than he had expected and tried to think of how to process the information so that he could deal with it.

That's it! He began to form the info-buffer into a virt-terminal which was in contact with the CPU.

He just typed on the keys; it didn't even need cards! He soon learned that the frequency pattern sequence had already been computed and was queuing up to return to the engineers.

Arvid had to act fast. He diverted the sequence toward the payroll banks to buy some time while he set about finding the key to controlling the bi- polar attenuator apparatus.

Sweat rolled down Rory Calhoun's bloated face, making the normally stalwart engineer resemble a drowned corpse.

“The GENIAC's on line but we aren't getting the frequency pattern sequence we need!” Calhoun snarled.

Glen MacDonald manipulated a slide rule with expert fingers. “My calculations show that GENIAC has the correct answer and that it should have come out of the output slot three and five-sixteenths minutes ago! A mutant factor must have entered the computations.”

His great brow furrowed. Slowly, the master engineer stood up and walked a few paces into the middle of the room, his hob-nailed boots echoing on the cold steelio plates of the floor.

MacDonald stared into space for a few seconds. The others watched in silence. They knew that Glen MacDonald was deep in thought. Not ordinary thought alone, however, for long ago in the giant engineer's youth, when he was only an orphaned beggar boy wandering the streets of some nameless Eastern bazaar, he had been processed into the esoteric mental discipline of Second Order Nexialism! Second Order Nexialism! A higher form of null-Aristotelian analysis, combined with the most rigorous and searching form of General Semantics. Trained to see the map, not the territory, leaping far ahead of suppressive categorical imperatives, MacDonald's canny Scots mind flashed from solution to inference!

“Calculations...computer...sequence...modern youth...door left ajar on fifth floor...the mutant factor is...” But MacDonald was unable to complete the inexorable Nexialist thought-map.

A pounding at the huge steelio doors at the far end of the chamber broke his concentration, shattered the crystal purity of his inferential sequence.

“Fungo!” shouted Rory Calhoun. “What motherless ballpeen could be coming here at this hour?”

“I think I know,” replied MacDonald in an even voice. “Jim, punch in the door-opening sequence. We've got visitors.”

The massive, five foot thick twin halves of the steelio doors slowly rotated down into the floor. Overhead spotlights revealed a small group of men in white lab coats, striding purposefully toward the group of engineers. Glen McDonald instantly recognized the thin, ascetic form of Dr. Felix Mahlstadt, followed by Mike Kopachevsky, Joe Wang, and Erich Vandenloos, the mathematician. Following them was Arlene Cooper, clutching a briefcase.

“I thought I would find you all here,” said Dr. Mahlstadt. “Excellent! That makes it more convenient to inform all of you at once that this project has been terminated, and to begin shutdown and dismantlement procedures immediately.”

“Is it 'convenient' for mankind, Doctor?” said Jim Harcourt. “Do your seminars and symposia hold as much promise for Man's future as our single candle against the darkness of ignorance and want? Can't you see the truth of what we might accomplish here tonight? Will you let it be stillborn?”

After a brief silence, in which the distant walls seemed to ring with Harcourt's words, Mike Kopachevsky cleared his throat. “It's all a lot of nonsense” he rumbled, “a complete waste of time and valuable resources. This facility should have been turned over long ago to myself for our muon-permittivity research. And here it is being used for you boys trying to square the circle!”

“Actually” murmured Vandenloos in a diffident manner to nobody in particular, “I think it would make an ideal setting for Snack Shop 13. It's so stifling in 5 that we can't get anything done. We could put in a swimming pool.”

Arlene Cooper took a notebook from her briefcase. “We've got a fascinating sewage-treatment project in our Scrub and Sludge division, Mr. MacDonald. I'm sure that would benefit mankind nearly as much as your very clever infinite power idea.”

Rory Calhoun stepped up to Kopachevsky. “Listen,” he grated through clenched teeth. “you scientists burn my fundament! If you want to shut us down, you have to be man enough to pass the test!”

The scientists looked at each other incredulously. “What kind of crazy test are they talking about, Mike-O?” asked Joe Wang.

“A man. Four in the morning, two at noon and three at night. Now shut it off, you buffoons,” Felix snapped.

Rory seemed taken aback for a moment. “Well, that's only test number one!” he shouted. “You sheet chewers can't pass test number two!”

The rest of the engineers began to chant “Test number two! Test number two!” “Hitsies” snarled Rory. “First man to turn ballpeen loses and goes away and never bothers us again!”

Felix walked toward the autovidphone. “This is even more mindless than I gave you engineers credit for. I'm calling security.”

Glen MacDonald's grey eyes narrowed. He stepped over to the control panel, flipped open a secret breaker bar. Red lights began to flash as MacDonald laid one great hand on a yellow switch marked DESTRUCT 3.

“Put down that vidciever.” he said. “This switch controls the main power flux tensors into this facility. One click and we'll all be pushing up Astroturf.”

Felix slowly put down the vidceiver. “You're completely psychotic, MacDonald. Naturally, I had always suspected it from your writings but it is rather unpleasant to have it confirmed in so melodramatic a fashion.”

“Crazy, Dr. Mahlstadt?” MacDonald laughed easily."Or saner than anybody’s been on this crazy ball of mud for the past five million years!”

“Crazy. Definitely,” muttered Wang under his breath.

“Uh...gee, Glen, couldn’t we maybe shut down for a little while or something...” Rory’s face had turned an ugly slate color.

Meanwhile, unnoticed by anyone else, Vandenloos had slipped out of the room.

The mathematician was walking briskly down the corridor in the opposite direction of any possible explosion.

If I had remained, he reasoned, I could hardly have prevented anything from happening, and since my presence can not actually help the situation, and since my safety is the primary objective in any rational scheme of action, leaving is the most legitimate strategy in this situation. Furthermore, he thought, any sad accident occuring to the engineering and scientific staff of Technicorp might easily free up needed funds for the mathematics division, and, in the absence of the only other leading mathematician in the company, Felix Mahlstadt, I would be very badly needed to head the mathematics division in such an event. Vandenloos whistled the “Ode To Joy” under his breath, and his step fell a little lighter the further away from Energylab he got.

As he passed by one door, he noticed that the light was on. From inside the room he could hear, above the roar of GENIAC, the faint but familiar sounds of “Lesbian Death Dirge” by Tesco Vee.

Vandenloos hesitated for a moment, then pushed the door open and stepped inside.

The first thing he noticed was a strong smell of carbon tetrachloride. The second thing was a skinny and extremely dirty young man lying on the floor in a puddle of drool, wearing an outlaw virt- harness hooked up to the GENIAC terminal. The third and fourth things were Phil and Denise.

Denise’ eyes widened and she backed away.

Phil jerked his head out of the bucket of carbon tetrachloride, saw Vandenloos, and passed out.

“Good evening” said Vandenloos politely. “Could you please turn the music down? My name is Erich Vandenloos.”

Denise turned off the tape player. “Uh...hi.” she said, managing a weak smile.

“Hi.” said Vandenloos. “You might want to leave the building, as I believe that a large explosion might occur at any moment. By the way, isn’t that fellow wearing one of the well-known outlaw virt-harnesses? Why is he exploring the GENIAC virt-world? It’s so stupid.”

“We...we can’t leave!” Denise gasped. “Arvid’s on the wire! He’ll go into ECG-shock if we pull him out now.”

Vandenloos inspected Arvid, frowning. “Can’t imagine he has much of a pattern to disrupt.” Vandenloos pulled the harness off, causing Arvid’s hapless body to go into convulsions. “I think I’ll take a look around, if you don’t mind.” he continued, putting on the harness.

Arvid’s fingers flew across the virt-key punch keys of the virt- terminal.

Not long now. The last linkages were in place for direct control of the subatomic energy facility! Soon GENIAC would directly control the power of millions of exploded sub-atomic universes! Lots of power! From the virt-world he could then expand GENIAC’s control via electromagnetic induction into every computer on the planet. One Big Net! Free long-distance autovidphone calls and pizzarine deliveries for all the Kids! The Master Anarchy Plan! His virt-fingers reached for the last virt keys!

“Stop!” cried a tiny voice from...somewhere. It sounded like Denise’s voice but through a hyper-aluminum pipe. Arvid looked around, but everything was losing its coherency, was turning into a pandemonium of colors and voices, and he knew he was being forced off-line.

He felt his own body come back suddenly...too suddenly, and Arvid felt nothing more.

“No, Rory,” Glen shook his head. “You know what we have to do. We’ve all talked real brave and said alot of high-sounding words, but if we back down now that’s all it’ll be, words.”

“Listen, MacDonald,” Kopachevsky exclaimed. “If it will keep you from blowing the whole funging lab up, I’ll take up your challenge! Bring on Test number two, if you must.”

“No, it has to be Mahlstadt,” Jim stated. “He’s the top-dog in your little group.”

“If you think I’m going to let those degenerates touch me, Mike, you’re as crazy as they are!” Felix screamed, making his way back toward the other scientists.

“Gentlemen,” offered Arlene with desperation in her voice, “I’m sure we can come to an agreement here. Dr. Mahlstadt, how about if you let them have tonight to finish their project, and if it doesn’t work, then shut it down in the morning!”

Calhoun said “Hey, Glen! We can do it! We almost had it before they showed up! That’s a fair offer!”

“Yeah! Felix, there’s no way these crazy goobers can do it! It’s a great plan,” Wang chimed in.

At that moment, Kopachevsky lunged at Glen, knocking him away from the DESTRUCT 3 switch. “I’ve had just about enough of this!” he yelled and disengaged the switch.

Glen’s eyes narrowed. His lips pulled back from his teeth in a snarl. His mighty fist swung in a haymaker that connected with Kopachevesky’s jaw. Kopachevsky staggered back, then delivered a tremendous blow to the giant Scot’s stomach.

The others stood back, struck with awe at the sight of the two huge men trading blow after blow! The burly Slav slammed Glen MacDonald into a bank of oscillating ontological accumulators, sending showers of sparks flying as the axiomatic switches shorted out.

MacDonald got to his feet and picked up a Fourier table, heaving the giant apparatus at Kopachevsky, who jumped aside just before it crashed through the floor where he had been standing, smashed through three more floors below and finally crushed a Koffee machine in the sub-basement.

A geyser of Koffee shot up through the hole, drenching Mahlstadt and the rest. Slipping in a puddle of Kreem, MacDonald hurled himself at Kopachevsky, who smashed a large ceramik insulator over Glen’s head.

Glen paused for a second, shook his head, and kept coming.

Once again the two giants traded punches, amid smoke, sparks, and a fine mist of Koffee.

Above them all towered the bi-polar attenuator apparatus, whining and shaking ominously, great arcs of electromagnetic energy playing about the attenuator poles.

Vandenloos looked at the virt-terminal and frowned.

Picking up a virt-pencil, he quickly sketched a passable duplicate of his own office in the virt space, using his desk as a “metaphor” for the new interface. The Mickey Mouse phone was output, the “in” box was input, the desk calculator was a calculator, and the various papers and candy bar wrappers on the desk stood for different memory segments of GENIAC.

He picked up his virt-paperweight and shook it. Looking inside gave an excellent view of Energylab, once the virt-snow had cleared. Apparently some sort of fight had broken out between Kopachevsky and MacDonald, who were now hurling file cabinets at each other. He looked for Mahlstadt, and noted with satisfaction that he was cowering behind a generator with the others. Something brown was raining down on everybody, and the large complicated device in the middle of the room was beginning to shake itself apart.

Vandenloos put a tape of Brahms’ Opus 25 Piano Quartet into his virt-8 track tape player, and began looking through the “papers” on his “desk”. Within milliseconds, he had grasped the complete extent both of the engineer’s energy project and of the GENIAC operating system.

Infinite energy, of course, was of no interest to Vandenloos. However, he noticed that the ability of the engineer’s machine to reach into the subatomic multiverses might be useful. If, he thought, making a few hasty sums on his “desk calculator”, one considered the subatomic suns as “on-off” switches, one could very easily construct a digital calculating machine of infinite computational power.

He picked up the Mickey Mouse phone and gave a few direct commands to GENIAC, rerouting the subatomic matrix in a matter of nanoseconds. Quickly looking up the few basic axioms in his virt-library, he programmed the resultant supercomputer to his own purpose...

Nearly exhausted, Kopachevsky swung the big condenser by it’s cable into MacDonald’s head.

The condenser shattered on MacDonald’s iron jaw, spattering PVCs everywhere. MacDonald blinked his eyes, then drew back one piledriver-like fist for another punch.

With a sudden groan and a thud, the bi-polar attenuator stopped shaking and giving off smoke. It settled into a soft whirring sound.

MacDonald paused, looking at the great machine.

So did Kopachevsky, dropping the drum of depleted uranium he had picked up.

The geyser of Koffee subsided. Cautiously, Felix Mahlstadt and the rest of the scientists and engineers emerged from behind the generator. Arlene came with them, clinging to Felix’s arm.

“Energy production!” boomed Glen in triumph. “There, Mahlstadt, there’s your proof! Energy from subatomic annihilation!”

“Nonsense” said Felix weakly “It is still a lot of nonsense.”

“But Glen,” said Jim “we never sent it the last code! It can’t be running!”

Glen strode over to the control panel. He looked at the dials. “It is running” he said slowly. “It’s...it’s operating directly from GENIAC. But...but Second Order Nexialism clearly contraindicates direct computer control. It’s a mutant concept!”

“You’re a mutant concept,” quipped Joe Wang. “That crazy Second Order Nexialism was discredited at Santa Cruz years ago!”

“Exactly correct, Wang” added Mahlstadt. “Now will you admit failure, MacDonald? Your machine runs, but it produces nothing!”

Someone behind them cleared his throat.

“Excuse me,” said Erich Vandenloos “but I must disagree, at least partly, with both of you.” They turned and saw the mathematician behind them. He was followed by Denise, who was supporting a comatose Arvid Schiller. Schiller was wearing a virt-harness attached to a small metal box attached to the small of his back. His eyes were rolled up into his head.

Erich motioned Denise to put Schiller in a convenient chair.

“Where have you been, Vandenloos?” said Mahlstadt, with a touch of irritation.

“Down the hall,” said Vandenloos “working on all our problems. I mean that in an extremely general sense.”

“What have you done to our computer linkage” growled MacDonald. “Are you responsible for sabotaging our project?”

“It’s self-evidently impossible to sabotage a project that is impossible to begin with” replied Vandenloos. “I might well add, that my modifications to your system have transformed a rather tedious technical problem into a fairly interesting result.”

“What are you talking about, Erich?” demanded Kopachevsky, idly picking up a steelio bar. “You math fellows are incessant obscurantists!”

“It’s obvious,” continued Vandenloos, oblivious to both MacDonald’s and Kopachevsky’s threatening looks, “that given a computing engine of sufficient complexity, working from a few simple axioms, it would be possible to construct a Universal Truth Machine. This machine, of course, would have the answer to any possible scientific and technical question, or indeed, the answer to any possible question at all. I have constructed such a machine, using GENIAC to control calculations on the subatomic level through your machine, Mr. MacDonald. I felt that this would be a much more worthwhile use of your technology than some absurd infinite power scheme.”

Vandenloos walked over to Arvid, who was slumped over in the chair. “My assistant here suffered an unfortunate accident, during which the patterns of his brain were completely wiped clean. Although this would be an handicap in any other future career, it admirably suits him for use as an interface for my Universal Truth Machine.”

Rory challenged Vandenloos. “Have it compute pi to one thousand places!”

Vandenloos frowned. He spoke into a microphone attached to Arvid’s lolling head.

Arvid began to drone: “Three point one four one five nine...”

Arvid continued for some minutes, while Rory kept up on his slide rule. At the end, Rory shook his head in amazement.

Kopachevsky sighed in disgust, then stepped forward. “Is every even number the sum of two odd primes?” he rumbled. “Yes.” came the answer from Arvid’s blue lips.

“Hey,” said Joe Wang, “can he tell me where I lost my copy of ‘Don Quixote’?”

“On the bus.” came the answer.

Felix Mahlstadt came forward, a half-smile on his lips. “Very amusing, Vandenloos. I admire the neurotic quality of your humor, but this joke has gone far enough. Have your Universal Truth Machine prove this sentence: I cannot be proven by the Universal Truth Machine.”

In a few short, well-chosen sentences, the answer to Mahlstadt’s question was quickly given, in a proof unmistakably correct and complete, even elegant.

“As you can see, my Universal Truth Machine works perfectly,” said Vandenloos, as Denise tried to keep Arvid from falling off the chair, “and, as long as the interface remains operational, it can answer any possible question. In the event, I don’t imagine that replacement of the interface would present much of a problem - the eager youth of our society would, I think, provide many suitable subjects. However, a problem does arise. Since my UTM (as I will now proceed to call it) can answer any possible question, it seems to me that the need for the services of scientists and engineers, (or even, alas, mathematicians) might be very greatly reduced.”

The scientists and engineers looked at each other.

“I suggest, therefore, cooperation, not conflict. We control GENIAC, and we control the bi-polar attenuator.” Vandenloos paused. “It seems to me that not only could we place ourselves at the head of our professions, but that, using the answers provided by the UTM, have a considerable, if not decisive influence on the conduct of society at large.”

There was silence for a moment. “Of course,” said Glen MacDonald, “it would be for the advancement of mankind’s destiny.”

“I think we can all agree,” said Felix Mahlstadt, “that the constraints of unthinking bureaucracy have been bad for the progress of science.”

“I think it’s a great idea.” said Arlene brightly “and I’m sure you’ll need a good PR worker to help things along!”

Everybody brightened when they thought of the possibilities of helping mankind.

Mahlstadt and MacDonald shook hands. Rory broke out another case of cheap, evil smelling beer.

At that moment, Davis Fenster appeared at the doorway. He walked up to the cheerful group of scientists and engineers. His face turned red as he saw the damage to the lab equipment. “What’s going on here?” he demanded angrily.

Everybody laughed.
